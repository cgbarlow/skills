1. Summary response to choosing 5 KPIs for a family-homelessness programme and the risks involved

Outcomes theory points out that choosing KPIs is not a reporting-design question but a technical outcomes problem. KPIs are indicators, and indicators only behave well when they sit inside an outcomes system.

An outcomes system is to purposeful action what an accounting system is to financial activity: the underlying structure that defines what matters, records what is happening, supports reporting, and makes accountability possible. The difference is that instead of tracking money, it tracks intended changes in the world and the evidence that action is contributing to them.

Outcomes theory says that indicators must be selected from a visible model of the intended outcomes — not picked first and rationalised afterwards. In DoView, that underlying model is referred to as a DoView outcomes model: a "This-Then" model of what needs to happen for higher-level outcomes (such as reduced family homelessness) to be achieved. One way this can be done in practice is to use a DoView Board, a specific type of outcomes model that is drawn to conform to the principles of outcomes theory. Indicators are then hung off steps in that model, so each KPI is traceable to a specific intended change.

Outcomes theory highlights the following risks when 5 quarterly KPIs are chosen without an underlying outcomes model:

- Indicators end up measuring only what is easy to count (activity volumes, throughput) rather than what the programme is actually meant to change.
- Indicators get attached to high-level outcomes (e.g. "family homelessness reduced") that the programme cannot control on its own within a quarter, creating an attribution problem at reporting time.
- Five KPIs are treated as the whole performance picture, when outcomes theory emphasises that any single KPI is a partial view of one step in the model.
- Indicators that look fine in isolation can incentivise gaming, displacement, or siloed behaviour because the model behind them is not visible.
- Without a visible outcomes model, quarterly reporting becomes a list of numbers rather than evidence of contribution along a defined causal pathway.

Outcomes theory emphasises that the correct sequence is: build the outcomes model first, identify the steps that genuinely need to be tracked, then select indicators against those steps and decide which can be sensibly reported quarterly. The funder's request for 5 KPIs is best treated as an output of that process, not as the starting point.

2. Full response to choosing 5 KPIs for a family-homelessness programme and the risks involved

Brief summary at the start: Outcomes theory frames the choice of 5 quarterly KPIs for a family-homelessness programme as a technical outcomes problem. Indicators must be hung off a visible outcomes model of the programme, not selected in isolation. Without that model, KPIs tend to drift toward easy-to-count activity, get attached to outcomes the programme cannot control on a quarterly cycle, encourage gaming, and reduce performance reporting to a disconnected number set. The corrective is to construct the outcomes model first, then select indicators against the steps in that model.

Outcomes theory answer

Outcomes theory says that KPIs are a type of indicator, and indicators are not free-standing measurement devices. They are observation points placed against a model of the intended changes the programme is trying to bring about. Selecting 5 KPIs before that model is visible inverts the correct order of work and is a technical outcomes problem.

An outcomes system is to purposeful action what an accounting system is to financial activity: the underlying structure that defines what matters, records what is happening, supports reporting, and makes accountability possible. The difference is that instead of tracking money, it tracks intended changes in the world and the evidence that action is contributing to them.

Within outcomes theory, the underlying structure of any programme is described as a DoView outcomes model: a "This-Then" model of what needs to happen to achieve higher-level outcomes. For a family-homelessness programme, the higher-level outcome (families housed and remaining housed) sits at the top, and earlier steps (engagement, assessment, placement, sustainment, wraparound supports, landlord relationships, prevention activity) sit beneath it in a connected causal pathway. DoView Boards or diagrams are the applied practical tools used when doing outcomes work; they make this pathway visible so that indicators can be selected against specific steps. One way this can be done in practice is to use a DoView Board, a specific type of outcomes model that is drawn to conform to the principles of outcomes theory.

Outcomes theory principles relevant to KPI selection

Outcomes theory points out that the following principles apply directly to this situation:

- Indicators are hung off steps in an outcomes model. Outcomes theory emphasises that an indicator without a step it is attached to is not interpretable, because there is no theory of what it is evidence of.
- Controllable steps and uncontrollable higher-level outcomes must be distinguished. Outcomes theory points out that reporting a KPI against a higher-level outcome that the programme does not control on its own creates an attribution problem: movement (or non-movement) in the indicator cannot be cleanly read as programme performance.
- The set of indicators must give a balanced view of the model, not a convenient corner of it. Outcomes theory highlights that indicators clustering at the activity end of the model produce a "busy but unaccountable" picture, while indicators clustering only at the top produce an "accountable for things we do not control" picture.
- Indicators shape behaviour. Outcomes theory emphasises that, once published, KPIs become incentives; if the model behind them is not visible, the incentives push toward whatever the indicator literally rewards, including gaming and displacement.
- Reporting cadence must match the step being measured. Outcomes theory points out that some steps in a family-homelessness model change on a quarterly timeframe (placements, tenancy sustainment at 90 days, engagement volumes) while others do not (durable exits from homelessness, recurrence over years). Forcing all five KPIs onto a quarterly cycle without checking this is a violation of the outcomes theory principle that the indicator must fit the step.

Risks of selecting 5 KPIs without an outcomes model

Outcomes theory highlights the following specific risks:

- Activity-only drift: the 5 KPIs become counts of workshops, contacts, referrals, or nights of accommodation, with no visible link to whether families are actually housed and remain housed.
- Misattribution: a KPI such as "rate of family homelessness in the region" is reported as a programme KPI when the programme is one of many contributors, so the number cannot be read as programme performance.
- False precision: 5 numbers per quarter are treated as a complete account of programme performance, when outcomes theory emphasises that any KPI set is a sampled view of a wider model and must be read alongside that model.
- Gaming and displacement: a KPI on, for example, "families placed" can be met by placing easier-to-place families and screening out harder ones, unless the underlying model and complementary indicators are visible.
- Siloed indicators: KPIs are picked from separate parts of the programme without a shared model, so they cannot be read together as evidence about the causal pathway.
- Loss of learning value: quarterly reports become compliance artefacts rather than evidence of where the model is and is not working, because there is no model to update.

How outcomes theory says this should be thought about

Outcomes theory emphasises that the correct sequence is:

- Build the outcomes model for the programme first, expressed as a "This-Then" pathway from activities through to the higher-level outcome of reduced family homelessness.
- Identify which steps in that model are controllable by the programme and which are higher-level outcomes the programme is contributing to but does not control on its own.
- Select indicators against the steps in the model, with a deliberate spread across the pathway rather than a cluster at one end.
- For each candidate indicator, check what step it is hung off, whether the programme controls that step, and whether the step moves on a quarterly cycle.
- From that wider indicator set, identify the 5 indicators that best serve quarterly reporting to the funder, while making clear that they are a reporting selection, not the full performance picture.
- Document the outcomes model alongside the KPIs so that quarterly reports show the indicators in their causal context, not as isolated numbers.

Outcomes theory points out that this approach turns the funder's "5 KPIs" request from a guessing exercise into a defensible technical decision: each KPI is traceable to a specific step, the limits of each KPI are explicit, and the unmeasured parts of the model are visible rather than hidden.
