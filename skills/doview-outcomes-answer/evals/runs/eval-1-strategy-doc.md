Outcomes theory points out that the document as presented is a vision statement plus a list of priorities and an investment figure, but it is not an outcomes system. Outcomes theory says that an outcomes system is to purposeful action what an accounting system is to financial activity: the underlying structure that defines what matters, records what is happening, supports reporting, and makes accountability possible. The difference is that instead of tracking money, it tracks intended changes in the world and the evidence that action is contributing to them. Outcomes theory points out that this is a technical outcomes problem because the document jumps from a high-level vision directly to a list of priority labels and a budget, without an explicit underlying model of what needs to happen to produce those higher-level outcomes. There is no visible chain showing how investment in housing affordability, climate resilience and vibrant neighbourhoods is intended to bring about a thriving, inclusive city, and no specification of the lower-level steps under each priority.

Outcomes theory emphasises that priorities such as “housing affordability,” “climate resilience” and “vibrant neighbourhoods” are outcome-level labels, not steps. Outcomes theory highlights that placing measurement (community surveys and progress reports) directly under such labels, with no intermediate steps, violates the outcomes theory principle that steps must not be siloed under outcomes and that indicators must be located on an explicit strategy or outcomes diagram. Outcomes theory points out that community surveys and generic progress reports are not, on their own, an indicator framework: they have not been derived from a model of the initiative’s outcomes and so cannot demonstrate that the $50m investment is contributing to the stated higher-level outcomes.

2. Full response to what is wrong, from an outcomes theory perspective, with a council strategy document that states a vision, three priorities, a $50m three-year investment and measurement via community surveys and progress reports, and how it should be restructured

Brief summary at the start: outcomes theory points out that the council document is a vision plus a label list plus a budget plus a default measurement promise, with no explicit outcomes model connecting them. Outcomes theory says that this is a technical outcomes problem because the higher-level outcomes (a thriving, inclusive city; housing affordability; climate resilience; vibrant neighbourhoods) have not been broken down into the lower-level outcomes and controllable steps that produce them, and because indicators (community surveys and progress reports) have been chosen before an outcomes model exists. Restructuring requires building an explicit outcomes model and locating indicators on that model. DoView is a practical applied form of outcomes theory and provides the diagrammatic and procedural tools to do this.

Outcomes theory points out that the council document conflates several distinct components of an outcomes system. An outcomes system is to purposeful action what an accounting system is to financial activity: the underlying structure that defines what matters, records what is happening, supports reporting, and makes accountability possible. The difference is that instead of tracking money, it tracks intended changes in the world and the evidence that action is contributing to them. In the council document, the vision functions as a top-level outcome, the three priorities function as mid- to high-level outcome categories, the $50m functions as input, and the community surveys and progress reports function as proposed indicators and reporting mechanisms. Outcomes theory emphasises that these components only become an outcomes system when they sit inside a single explicit model of what needs to happen to achieve higher-level outcomes.

Outcomes theory talks in terms of a DoView outcomes model underlying action in the world: a “This-Then” model of what needs to happen to achieve higher-level outcomes. In a “This-Then” model, lower-level controllable steps lead to higher-level, often less controllable, outcomes. The council document does not contain such a model. It moves directly from “Our Vision” to three priority labels to dollars to measurement, with no statement of the intermediate steps or lower-level outcomes that would have to occur for housing affordability, climate resilience or vibrant neighbourhoods to be realised, and no statement of how those three priorities, if realised, would in turn produce a thriving, inclusive city.

Outcomes theory points out that this is a technical outcomes problem. The three priorities are outcome-level labels, not steps, and outcomes theory highlights that placing measurement and budget directly under outcome-level labels with no intermediate structure violates the outcomes theory principle that steps must not be siloed under outcomes. This is the principle articulated in the Do Not Silo Steps Under Outcomes Explainer. Outcomes theory also points out that selecting indicators (community surveys, progress reports) in advance of an outcomes model violates the principle that indicators are located onto a strategy or outcomes diagram, not chosen as generic defaults. Community surveys may yield perception data and progress reports may yield activity data, but neither has been derived from an outcomes model and so neither can be relied upon to show whether the $50m investment is contributing to the stated higher-level outcomes.

Outcomes theory highlights that the components named in the document map onto specific parts of the DoView Planning Framework: vision and priorities correspond to higher-level outcomes; the missing material corresponds to lower-level outcomes and controllable steps; the $50m corresponds to inputs; community surveys and progress reports correspond, at best, to a partial and pre-emptive indicator selection. Outcomes theory emphasises that a complete outcomes system requires all of these components to be visible together and connected.

One way this can be done in practice is to use a DoView Board, a specific type of outcomes model that is drawn to conform to the principles of outcomes theory. DoView Boards or diagrams are applied practical tools used when doing outcomes work. They are drawn so that higher-level outcomes appear at the top, lower-level outcomes and controllable steps appear underneath, and the “This-Then” linkages between them are made explicit. Indicators are then placed onto the diagram against the specific steps or outcomes they are intended to evidence, and the suitability of deliverables, outputs and KPIs is checked against the model.

Practical formal implications of restructuring the council document along outcomes theory lines:

First, the vision “a thriving, inclusive city” is retained as the top-level outcome, but is paired with an explicit statement of what would count as evidence of such a city, derived from a model rather than from a default survey instrument.

Second, each of the three priorities — housing affordability, climate resilience and vibrant neighbourhoods — is treated as a high-level outcome that requires its own underlying “This-Then” structure. Under each priority, the lower-level outcomes and the controllable steps that the council intends to take with the $50m are made explicit. This addresses the siloing problem identified in the Do Not Silo Steps Under Outcomes Explainer.

Third, the $50m investment is allocated against the controllable steps in the diagram rather than against the priority labels, so that it is visible which steps are funded and which are not. This is consistent with the requirement that an initiative’s outcomes are defined before resources are committed.

Fourth, the indicator set is rebuilt by placing indicators onto the strategy or outcomes diagram. Community surveys, if retained, are placed against the specific higher-level outcomes or perception-related outcomes that they actually evidence. Progress reports are restructured around the steps and lower-level outcomes shown in the diagram. The full indicator list is then checked using a suitability checklist and reviewed as a list, rather than accepted as a generic default.

Fifth, the document as a whole is reframed so that the vision, priorities, budget and measurement appear as parts of a single outcomes system, with the underlying outcomes model visible to readers. This makes it possible to report against the model in subsequent years and to test whether the $50m has contributed to the stated higher-level outcomes.
